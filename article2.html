What is Utilitarianism?

The basic idea

Utilitarianism is a moral theory that asks a simple but powerful question: what will lead to the best overall outcome?

Instead of focusing mainly on rules, intentions, or traditions, utilitarianism evaluates actions based on their consequences—especially how much happiness or suffering they produce.

Consequences matter

At its core, utilitarianism is a form of consequentialism. This means that the rightness or wrongness of an action depends on its results.

In its classic form, utilitarianism says we should act in ways that maximize overall well-being for everyone affected.

Peter Singer’s challenge

Singer argues that if we can prevent suffering without sacrificing anything of comparable importance, then we are morally required to do so.

His drowning child example makes this idea feel immediate: if you would save a child drowning nearby, even at the cost of ruining your clothes, why should distance change your moral responsibility?

Why people find it appealing

Utilitarianism is powerful because it treats everyone’s well-being as equally important. It focuses on real-world outcomes: reducing suffering, improving lives, and making choices that create the most good.

Challenges

But utilitarianism also raises difficult questions. Can it demand too much from us? Could it justify harming one person to help many others? Does it leave enough room for family, friendship, rights, and personal commitments?

The deeper question

Utilitarianism remains influential because it forces us to take suffering seriously. It asks us not only what feels right, but what actually makes the world better.
