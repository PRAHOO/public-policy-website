Pranav's Perspective

Is utilitarianism actually right?

In my opinion, the core of utilitarianism is basically correct. At a very fundamental level, it feels almost impossible to deny that an action which produces more good than another is the better action.

Singer’s argument pushes this intuition in a way that is hard to escape. If we accept the obligation to save a drowning child when the cost to us is small, then it becomes difficult to explain why distance should matter morally.

The biggest problem: measuring good and bad

My main issue with utilitarianism is not the principle itself, but the fact that it assumes we can compare and measure different outcomes.

In real life, we often cannot assign exact values to suffering, happiness, death, rights, or personal relationships. So even if utilitarianism gives us the right framework, applying it is much harder.

The integrity problem

Bernard Williams argues that utilitarianism can ignore personal integrity. It may treat people as points where consequences happen, rather than as individuals with commitments and responsibilities.

I understand why this objection feels important, but I do not find it fully persuasive. If the outcome is clearly better overall, then personal discomfort may not be enough to show that the utilitarian answer is wrong.

The demandingness problem

Where utilitarianism becomes genuinely difficult is in how demanding it is. If we take Singer seriously, helping others is not just admirable—it may be morally required whenever the cost to us is relatively small.

This is uncomfortable because it suggests that most people, including myself, probably do far less than morality requires.

Theory versus practice

In theory, utilitarianism suggests that there is always a right answer: the action that maximizes overall good. In practice, we act with limited information, uncertainty, and imperfect judgment.

Because of this, utilitarianism may work better as a guiding principle than as a strict decision-making algorithm.

Final take

Overall, I think utilitarianism is the strongest theoretical framework for morality because it focuses directly on outcomes and well-being.

At the same time, it is difficult to fully implement. We cannot measure utility precisely, we do not have complete information, and we are not capable of constantly making perfect moral decisions.

In the end, I see utilitarianism as fundamentally correct in principle, but very difficult to practice perfectly.
